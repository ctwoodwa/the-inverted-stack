The chapter makes a case. The case is also one a skeptic could engage with on its own terms. That is rarer than it sounds. The 8×4 table at lines 124–133 is the chapter's strongest argumentative artifact because it gives the skeptic a falsifiable structure: if the gap the author claims (vendor-independent data ownership ↔ team collaboration) does not in fact exist across the eight named systems, the reader can produce a counterexample. The chapter survives the test because the eight systems do, in fact, cluster as the table claims.

Where the case weakens: the chapter never engages with the strongest counterargument, which is not "Linear is good enough" but rather "the cost of the composition exceeds the benefit for 90% of buyers." A skeptic at McKinsey or Bain reads the chapter and asks: who pays for the composition, what does it cost them, and what is the deployed install base of any system that has actually delivered all seven properties at enterprise scale? The chapter's honest answer at line 36 ("To the authors' knowledge at time of writing, no production app satisfies all seven") is intellectually clean but commercially damning. The book is selling an architecture no one has shipped. The chapter needs to address that head-on, not bury it.

The differentiator triad at line 159 is the chapter's most exposed claim. "Three pieces no other published architecture combines" — but the per-record CAP boundary is a positioning, not a primitive (Riak and Cassandra both expose per-key consistency tunables; the novelty is the typed-record framing); the MDM-deployable installer is an engineering deliverable, not an architectural contribution; and the AGPLv3-with-managed-relay business model is a license/GTM choice that several open-source companies have made (GitLab, Mattermost, Sentry). The triad as currently stated is *defensible* but not *compelling*. A reviewer at a competitor will dismantle it in two sentences.

The regulatory framing at line 185 is the second most exposed claim. The chapter wants Schrems II + DPDP + DIFC DPL + 242-FZ to add up to "the regulatory pressure is now global." It does — but only if the reader already believes data localization is the binding constraint. A skeptic asks: why hasn't the SaaS market collapsed, then? Salesforce shipped €11B in EMEA last year. The chapter needs to acknowledge the gap between regulatory pressure and procurement reality.

**Action items:**
- Add one paragraph (around line 187) acknowledging the gap between regulatory exposure and procurement reality. The honest answer is: most enterprises tolerate Schrems II risk because the alternative architectures didn't exist. That answer strengthens the book's thesis.
- Replace the line-159 differentiator triad with a single load-bearing claim. Either the per-record CAP boundary OR the MDM-deployable installer OR the AGPLv3 business model — not all three as a list. A claim that hangs on the conjunction of three weak factors is weaker than a claim that hangs on one strong factor.
- The "no production app satisfies all seven" admission at line 36 is the chapter's most honest moment. Earn more from it. One sentence acknowledging that this means the book's architecture is *also* not yet shipped at all-seven completeness, and what the path to that is, would defuse a skeptic's strongest objection.
